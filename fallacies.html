FALLACIES

Every argument contains a premise and a conclusion. Premises are reasons or evidences that support a conclusion or claim. Fallacies occur when the premises do not adequately support the conclusion of the argument.
  • HASTY GENERALIZATION

When assumptions are made about a whole group based on an adequate sample; stereotyping.

Premise: Almost every Filipino in Hong kong is a domestic helper.

Conclusion: The Philippines is a nation of servants.

  • POST HOC (OR FALSE CAUSE)

When two events related in time are not related as cause and effect.

Premise: The mirror broke when my cat died.

Conclusion: A mirror breaking means a pet must die.

  • SLIPPERY SLOPE

When a chain reaction is presumed without enough evidence.

Premise: Graduates of good universities tend to land good jobs.

Conclusion: If I am not accepted in a good school, my education will be worthless. I won’t get a good job, and I will be poor and unhappy for the rest of my life.

  • WEAK ANALOGY

When two things are compared but the comparison is not relevant to the conclusion.

Premise: A cockroach is an animal, the same way man is an animal.

Conclusion: A cockroach must be treated with the same dignity as man.

  • APPEAL TO AUTHORITY

When an argument seems strong because the premise contains a respected authority.

Premise: Einstein believes God does not exist

Conclusion: God does not exist.

  • AD POPULUM (BANDWAGON)

When an argument seems strong because lots of people agree with it.

Premise: 60% percent of Americans think Barrack Obama is a great president.

Conclusion: Obama is a great president.

  • APPEAL TO PITY

When a premise appeals to a person’s feelings rather than to reason.

Premise: I was mistreated as a child and lived a hard life.

Conclusion: I should not be jailed for committing a crime.

  • APPEAL TO IGNORANCE

When an argument claims the opposite is true because there is not enough evidence to prove it is true.

Premise: Someone has yet to prove that aliens exist.

Conclusion: Aliens do not exist.
