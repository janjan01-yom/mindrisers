PARTS OF SPEECH

A. NOUN – is a word or a group of words used to name people, animals, places, things, or ideas.

KINDS OF NOUNS

Comon Noun

is used to name people, animals, places, things, or ideas in its general form. The first letter of these nouns are not capitalized.

Examples: teacher, dog, school, bag

Proper Noun

is used to specifically name a person, place, animal, thing, or idea. The first letter of these nouns is always capitalized.

Examples: Daniel, Philippines, Anello

Concrete Noun

is used to name people, places, animals, or things that can be perceived through the five senses whether directly or indirectly.

Examples: flower, whistling, aromas, troupe

Abstract Noun 

is used to name anything that cannot be perceived throught the five senses. These are usually ideas and emotions.

Examples: love, democracy, progress, anticipation

Count Noun 

is used to name objects that can be counted individually.

Examples: chair, book, phones, banana

Mass Noun 

is used to name objects that cannot be counted individually. To pluralize a mass noun, we add quantifiers such as kilo or bag or determiners (much or enough) before the mass noun.

Examples: water, sugar, smoke, rice

Collective Noun 

is used to refer to a group of people, places, animals, things or ideas.

Examples: a choir of singers, a flight of birds, a bowl of rice, an army of ants

Compound Noun 

is used to name anything that consists of two or more words put together. It can be written in solid, spaced, or hyphenated form.

Examples: eggplant, bus stop, mother-in-law

gender of nouns

Masculine Noun


is used to refer to nouns of the male kind.

Feminine Noun 


is used to refer to nouns of the female kind.

Common Noun 


is used to refer to nouns which may be either male or female.

Neuter Noun 


is used to refer to nouns that have no gender.

B. PRONOUN – is a word that substitutes a noun or a noun phrase.

 PERSONAL PRONOUNS – is distinguished by person:

  • First Person - refers to the speaker or writer
  • Second Person  - refers to the person or people being spoken or written to 
  • Third Person  - refers to the person or people being spoken or written about

CASES OF PRONOUNS


  • 1. Nominative Case – is used when the pronoun is part of the subject or the predicate nominative.

Examples:

We are celebrating a Christmas party. (subject)

Our science teacher is she. (predicate nominative)

  • 2. Objective Case – is used when the pronoun is part of:

a. The direct object (receiver of the action)

Jose accidentally kicked her on the shin while playing soccer.

b. The indirect object (receiver of the direct object; to/for whom the direct object is directed)

Portia bought me an ice cream.

c. The object of the preposition

He will not lie to me.

  • 3. Possessive Case – is used to show ownership.

The money in the bank is ours. (pronoun)

OTHER PRONOUNS

Demonstrative Pronouns

are used to point out a specific noun.

Relative Pronouns 

used to introduce adjective and noun clauses. Who and whom are used for persons, while which and that are used for non-persons.

Reflexive Pronouns 

are used when the subject also receives the action of the verb; followed by –self or –selves.

Intensive Pronouns 

used to show emphasis on the subject. Also followed by –self or –selves.

Indefinite Pronouns 

do not substitute for a specific noun.

Interrogative Pronouns 

are used to ask questions.

Reciprocal Pronouns 

are used to show an interchangeable action or relationship.

C. VERB - is used to denote action or a state of being

KINDS OF VERBS

  • 1. Action Verbs – shows physical or mental action.

Regular Verbs – form their past tense by adding –d or –ed at the end of the world.

Irregular Verbs – form their past tense by changing a letter, completely changing its form, or maintaining its original form.

  • 2. Linking Verbs – connects a subject to its compliment. Is, are, am, was, were, will/ would be, can/ could be, shall/ should be, and has/ have/ had been are commonly used linking verbs.
  • 3. Auxiliary Verbs/ Helping Verbs – used with the main verb to form verb phrase. Examples are shall, will, can, have, and must.
D. ADJECTIVES - used to modify a noun or pronoun

DESCRIPTIVE ADJECTIVES

Common Adjective 

tell the quality, color, taste, shape, or size.

Examples: hungry, unique, separate 

Proper Adjective 

are formed from a proper noun.

Examples: Philippine passport, Elizabethan drama 

Predicate Adjective 

comes after a linking verb.


Example: The melody is catchy. 

Compound Adjective 

are made of two adjectives joined by a hyphen.


Examples: English-speaking, eye-catching, well-known 

Possessive Adjective 

shows possession and are usually found before the noun described.

Examples: my, your, his, her, its, our, their 

DEGREE OF COMPARISON

  1.  Positive Degree – used when describing one noun
  2.  Comparative Degree – used when comparing two nouns
  3.  Superlative Degree – used when comparing three or more nouns
 ABSOLUTE ADJECTIVES – some adjectives cannot have varying degrees of comparison.
E. ADVERBS – modify a verb, an adjective, or another adverb.

KINDS OF ADVERBS

Adverbs of Time 


Example: We will visit the town tomorrow. 

Adverbs of Place


Example: Come here!

Adverbs of Manner


Example: Mara listens attentively.

Adverbs of Degree


Example: John is completely affected by the movie.

Adverbs of Affirmation and Negation


Example: I will certainly go to the party.

F. PREPOSITIONS 

are used to connect nouns to show their relationship with other words in the sentence.

G. CONJUNCTIONS – used to join words or group of words in a sentence. 

  • COORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS – join two words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance. 

Susie and Geno are part of the Sustagen team. (and is used to show similar ideas)

Are you going to get a phone or a laptop? (or is used to show a choice)

She promised to help, but she left early. (but is used to show contrasting ideas)

  • CORRELATIVE CONJUNCTIONS – also join words and phrases of equal importance, but act in pairs. 

Both Val and Tony are my siblings.

  • CONJUNCTIVE ADVERBS – are used to signal relationships between independent clauses.

The doctor helped me with my back problems; however, I still experience occasional pain.

  • SUBORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS - are used to connect a subordinate clause to a main clause. The two clauses form a complex sentence.

Although diabetes runs in the family, Jess still cannot resist chocolates.




H. INTERJECTIONS – used to convey emotions and are sometimes able to stand on their own in a sentence.

 Examples: wow, oh, hey, ouch, phew, jeepers, golly
